Model. Each step is a weighted coin-flip against the attacker's hashpower: win the flip and the attacker extends a private fork that pays the deposited coins back to itself; otherwise the honest network extends the public chain. The exchange releases funds once the deposit reaches the required confirmations. The attacker reveals the fork only once it is longer than the public chain, forcing a reorganization that orphans the deposit. Success estimate. The figure under the hashpower control is a Monte-Carlo estimate (thousands of runs of this same model) of how often the attack succeeds at the current settings — closely tracking Nakamoto's double-spend probability from the Bitcoin whitepaper. A minority attacker can still succeed over few confirmations, which is precisely why exchanges wait for many; at or above 50% hashpower, success becomes a matter of time. No signatures are forged — the attacker only ever rewrites its own transaction. Attacker patience is how many blocks behind the attacker keeps trying before abandoning the fork; raising it models a more patient attacker and pushes the estimate up toward the theoretical (infinitely patient) Nakamoto probability.
